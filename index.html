Reservoirs & Dams

Elan Valley

A spectacular Victorian engineering marvel featuring six interconnected reservoirs and dams set in 70 square miles of stunning Welsh countryside. The Craig Goch dam, with its elegant curved design, is the most photographed. Designated an International Dark Sky Park in 2015.

üìú History

Built between 1893-1904 to supply Birmingham with fresh water - an incredible 73 miles away, using gravity alone with no pumping required. The romantic poet Percy Bysshe Shelley lived at Nantgwyllt house, now submerged beneath the waters. Over 100 people were displaced when their homes were flooded.

üìç Visitor Info

Hours: Winter 10am-4pm (Summer 9am-5pm) | Tel: 01597 810880

Parking: Free first hour, ¬£3 daily, ¬£20 annual pass

Facilities: Visitor centre, cafe, toilets, EV charging (2 points), e-bike hire available

Forest

Hafren Forest

Deep in the Cambrian Mountains lies the source of Britain's longest river. A peaceful Sitka spruce forest with waymarked trails leading to the spring where the mighty Severn begins its 220-mile journey to the sea.

üìú History

"Hafren" is the Welsh name for the Severn, derived from Sabrina - the Celtic goddess of the river. Roman legions marched through here, and legend says Sabrina was a princess drowned by her jealous stepmother, transformed into the river goddess by water nymphs.

üìç Visitor Tips

The source is a 3-mile walk from the car park. Wear waterproof boots - it's boggy! The forest road is single-track. No facilities so bring snacks and drinks.

Bridge & Estuary

Barmouth Bridge

One of Britain's most spectacular railway crossings - a half-mile wooden viaduct stretching across the Mawddach estuary with Cadair Idris rising dramatically behind. You can walk across for incredible views in both directions.

üìú History

Built in 1867 for the Cambrian Railways, this Grade II* listed bridge is 820 metres long with 113 wooden spans. A metal swing section in the middle once allowed tall ships to pass. In 1980, marine worms threatened the structure - over 500 wooden piles were replaced in a ¬£4.3 million restoration. The bridge remains one of the oldest and longest wooden viaducts in regular use in Britain.

üìç Visitor Tips

¬£1 pedestrian toll (honesty box at Barmouth end). Walk takes 15-20 minutes each way. Best photos at sunset with Cadair Idris in the background. Car park in Barmouth town. The Mawddach Trail starts here for walkers/cyclists. üöÅ Incredible drone location - the bridge from above is spectacular!

Lake & Tower

Lake Vyrnwy

A Gothic fairytale in the Welsh hills. The Grade I listed straining tower rises from the lake like something from a storybook, designed to resemble a Bavarian castle. The 12-mile circuit around the lake offers spectacular views at every turn.

üìú History

Completed in 1888 to supply Liverpool with water, this was the first large stone-built dam in Britain. The village of Llanwddyn was completely submerged - 37 houses, 2 chapels, a church, 3 pubs and a post office lost beneath 13 billion gallons of water. Villagers were relocated to a new settlement. In dry summers, old ruins sometimes emerge.

üìç Visitor Tips

RSPB reserve with red squirrels and ospreys. Cafe at the dam. The sculpture trail around the lake is excellent. Best photos of the tower from the dam walkway. üöÅ Great drone location!

Waterfall

Pistyll Rhaeadr

Wales' tallest single-drop waterfall at 240 feet (73m), cascading dramatically through a natural rock arch. Listed as one of the Seven Wonders of Wales alongside Snowdon and the bells of Gresford.

üìú History

The Seven Wonders of Wales were celebrated in an 18th-century rhyme. Victorian travel writer George Borrow declared it "the finest waterfall in Britain" in his 1854 book "Wild Wales". The natural rock arch is called "The Fairy Bridge" - legend says fairies use it to cross the falls.

üìç Visitor Tips

Small cafe at the base (cash preferred). 20-minute climb to the top for valley views - steep but worth it! ¬£5 parking. The access road is very narrow - arrive early or late. üöÅ Spectacular drone shots possible!

Mountain

Cadair Idris

A legendary mountain rising to 893 metres (2,930 ft), with dramatic cliff faces, glacial lakes, and sweeping views across Snowdonia. One of Wales' most mystical peaks, steeped in folklore and Arthurian legend.

üìú History

"Cadair Idris" means "Chair of Idris" - named after the giant Idris Gawr who used the mountain as his throne to sit and watch the stars. Legend warns that anyone who sleeps on the summit will wake as either a poet or a madman. The crater lake Llyn Cau is said to be bottomless and home to a monster. King Arthur allegedly fought his final battle nearby.

üìç Visitor Tips

The Pony Path (5 miles round trip) is easiest. Minffordd route is steeper but more dramatic. Check weather before attempting - conditions change fast! Car park at Minffordd (pay & display). Allow 5-6 hours for summit and return.

Lake

Llyn Gwynant

A stunning glacial lake cradled between mountains in the heart of Snowdonia. On still mornings, the water creates perfect mirror reflections of the surrounding peaks - a photographer's paradise.

üìú History

"Gwynant" means "valley of the wind" in Welsh. The valley was carved by glaciers during the last Ice Age, around 10,000 years ago. Featured in the 2018 Tomb Raider film starring Alicia Vikander, standing in for a remote Siberian landscape.

üìç Visitor Tips

Campsite at the north end with toilets. Walk along the eastern shore for best views. Early morning is magical for photos when the water is still. üöÅ Excellent drone location - fly low over the reflections!

Village

Beddgelert

Often called "Snowdonia's prettiest village", Beddgelert sits where the rivers Glaslyn and Colwyn meet. Stone bridges, tea rooms, and mountain views make this a perfect stopping point.

üìú History

"Beddgelert" means "Grave of Gelert" - after the legendary hound of Prince Llywelyn the Great. The story goes that Llywelyn returned from hunting to find his baby's cradle overturned and Gelert with blood on his jaws. He killed the dog, only to then find his baby alive under the cradle - and a dead wolf that Gelert had killed protecting the child. Heartbroken, Llywelyn never smiled again. (Though historians suggest the tale was invented in 1793 to attract tourists!)

üìç Visitor Tips

Gelert's Grave is a short walk through the meadow. Glaslyn Ices is legendary - try the Welsh cake ice cream! The riverside walk is beautiful. Pubs serve good food. Paid parking in village centre.

Valley

Cwm Prysor

A wild and remote mountain valley with views of Trawsfynydd Lake. This is off-the-beaten-track Wales - dramatic scenery with few other visitors. The road back to Dolgellau is spectacular at sunset.

üìú History

Trawsfynydd was home to Wales' only inland nuclear power station, operational from 1965-1991. The Roman road "Sarn Helen" - possibly named after Helen of Caernarfon, wife of Emperor Magnus Maximus - passes through this ancient landscape, once connecting Roman forts across Wales.

üìç Visitor Tips

The narrow road has passing places - take it slow. No facilities so bring supplies. The nuclear power station is now being decommissioned - you can see it across the lake. Golden hour light here is incredible. üöÅ Remote = perfect drone flying!
